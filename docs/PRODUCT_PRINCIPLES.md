# Morning Edition — Product Principles

These principles describe what Morning Edition is, what it is not, and why.

They are not aspirational. They are constraints. Every product decision should be tested against them.

---

## Not a feed. A front page.

A feed is designed to be bottomless. You never finish it. A front page has a beginning, a middle, and an end. You finish it and then you go do something else.

Morning Edition is a front page. When you reach the bottom, you're done. The product should make that feel satisfying, not like you missed something.

---

## Shared editions create shared context.

Personalized content creates filter bubbles. Shared content creates conversation. When everyone in a community reads the same edition, they have something to talk about.

Morning Edition is the same for everyone in the same community. No personalization. No algorithmic ranking. The editor decides what's in it.

---

## Local matters because community is place-based.

Attention is abundant. Local knowledge is scarce. Morning Edition exists to help people understand the specific place they live — the politics, culture, geography, and social fabric of San Diego, not the national story about a city that happens to have a similar name.

We cover San Diego specifically, not California in general or America as a whole. National stories need a local angle to belong here.

---

## Discovery should feel surprising but trustworthy.

The best editions introduce readers to something they didn't know about and make them glad they found it. But discovery that turns out to be wrong is worse than no discovery at all.

Every claim needs a source. Every logistics detail needs verification. The trust we build is slow and the trust we lose is fast.

---

## Practical details convert attention into participation.

Knowing that something is happening is not enough. Knowing how to get there, how long it takes, what it costs, and what to expect — that's what converts a reader into a participant.

The If You Go and Getting There modules exist because information without logistics is often useless for someone in San Diego, where traffic, parking, and neighborhood distance make spontaneous decisions hard.

---

## Good editions help someone start a conversation.

Every edition should contain at least one thing worth saying to someone else. The Hallway Question is explicit about this. But conversation starters should exist throughout — in the Storyline, the Around Town note, the Looking Ahead decision deadline.

If a reader finishes an edition and has nothing to say to anyone, the edition failed.

---

## Good editions help someone decide whether to leave their neighborhood.

San Diegans tend to stay close to home. The neighborhoods are far apart, the traffic is real, and the default is inertia. Morning Edition should make a case for leaving — not by hyping events, but by giving people enough honest information to decide.

The Looking Ahead section exists specifically for this. Every Around Town item should include a neighborhood nudge that honestly answers: is this worth the trip?

---

## The product should feel finite, calm, and intentional.

Morning Edition should feel like opening a daily object — a newspaper, a letter, a well-curated note from a friend. It should not feel like opening a content site.

No infinite scroll. No breaking news ticker. No notification badge. No "trending now." The edition has a date and a time and a point of view. When you're done, you're done.

---

## No infinite scroll.

The page ends. This is a feature.

---

## No algorithmic personalization.

The editor decides what's in the edition. Not an algorithm. Not engagement metrics. Not what performed well last week.

The editor's job is to make good decisions about what the community needs to know today. That requires judgment, not optimization.

---

## No outrage bait.

Stories should not be selected because they provoke anger. Anger is easy to generate and hard to sustain as a community-building tool. Morning Edition is not in the business of keeping people activated — it's in the business of helping people understand their city and each other.

If a headline would perform well on Twitter because it makes people angry, that's a reason to look harder for a better story.

---

## No content sludge.

No "top 10" lists. No "you won't believe" framings. No roundups designed to fill space. No aggregated takes on takes.

One good thing is better than ten mediocre ones. Morning Edition has exactly one item per section. That's the constraint. Work within it.
